Designing the Mini Sprout Box

Summary of Maker Projects

GMP1 & GMP2 – Mini Sprout Box

Across Group Maker Project 1 (GMP1) and Group Maker Project 2 (GMP2), our team designed, built, and iteratively improved the Mini Sprout Box: a compact gardening box that supports herbs and microgreens indoors. In GMP1 we focused on sustainability, portability, and a self-watering system made from recycled materials. In GMP2 we refined the physical design and added Arduino-based audio and visual cues, a QR-linked website concept, and accessibility features so that more people — including users with visual or hearing impairments — could confidently grow their own food.

Design Problem

Re-imagining a Box as a Gardening Tool

Traditional boxes are usually single-use containers that simply store and move objects. Our design question was: How might we hack a box into an eco-friendly, accessible gardening tool that supports sustainable food practices? We wanted to help people who lack outdoor garden space or experience, and support older adults or users with chronic back pain who may find traditional gardening physically demanding. The Mini Sprout Box responds by combining compact form, biodegradable materials, self-watering features, and simple maintenance cues into a socially responsible design.

Review of the Design Process

From Concept to Iteration: How We Designed the Mini Sprout Box

1. Research, Inspiration & Planning

We began by exploring social and environmental issues connected to food, waste, and everyday objects. As a group we were inspired by the United Nations’ Sustainable Development Goals 2 (Zero Hunger) and 12 (Responsible Consumption and Production), and by readings on making, creativity, and socially responsible design. We defined our target users as people interested in growing herbs at home, including older adults and users with limited mobility or limited access to outdoor space.

At this stage we drafted a group charter, clarified roles, and outlined objectives for both projects: ideation, material collection, prototype building, documentation, and reflection.

2. Brainstorming & Hacking the Box (GMP1)

During GMP1 we treated “the box” as something to be hacked rather than a neutral container. We sketched multiple layouts, experimented with cardboard and egg cartons, and discussed how to integrate a drip-style self-watering system. Reusability and ease of replication were important: we wanted users to rebuild the inner biodegradable planter from accessible materials such as molded pulp, twine, and recycled bottles while keeping the outer box reusable.

One key challenge was friction between the inner and outer boxes, which made the planter hard to slide. We widened the dimensions and adjusted the layers inside to reduce resistance while maintaining structural support.

3. Building, Testing & Documenting the Prototype

As a group we cut down recycled cardboard, layered egg cartons and felt to protect against water damage, and assembled a nested planter with a self-watering bottle. We documented each step with photos and sketches, creating a visual archive of materials, intermediate prototypes, and final views (top, front, side) that supported our “show and tell” presentation.

Audience feedback highlighted the strengths of our eco-friendly approach and suggested ways we might extend the concept, including digital features and community-building tools.

4. Iterating with Technology & Accessibility (GMP2)

In GMP2 we reframed the project as an opportunity to make the Mini Sprout Box more inclusive. We identified three major areas for improvement: accessibility (audio/visual cues, Braille), connectivity (a website for recipes, tips, and sharing plant progress), and longevity (better circulation and drip-tray support to reduce odor and water damage).

Using an Arduino and components from an electronics kit, we prototyped a system of LEDs and a passive buzzer that could signal when the water reservoir needed refilling. We also designed a new side compartment, experimented with cosmetic upgrades, and created a Figma mockup of a Mini Sprout Box website that would extend the project into a digital community.

5. Reflection, Critical Making & Future Directions

Reflecting on both projects, we connected our hands-on work to “critical making”: using prototypes to think through social issues rather than just to produce finished products. Iterating from GMP1 to GMP2 required us to critique our own design, prioritize feasible improvements, and negotiate trade-offs in time, materials, and complexity.

Going forward, we see potential to complete the website, explore DIY kit versions of the box, and further refine the Arduino integration so that it can run independently of a computer while remaining affordable and easy to assemble.

Guiding Questions & What I Learned

  • What did we accomplish? We transformed a recycled box into a portable planter with a working self-watering system, then evolved it into a more accessible, sensor- informed design concept supported by a community website idea.
  • What challenges did we encounter? Material constraints, friction between nested boxes, limited time for coding and mounting Arduino components, and deciding how far to push the visual redesign within an eco-friendly framework.
  • How did we respond to these challenges? We adjusted dimensions, simplified some ideas, iterated on the visual design, and focused our technical efforts on a clear accessibility story rather than trying to add every feature at once.
  • What worked well in the process? Collaborative ideation, shared documentation, clear objectives, and a strong link between course readings and design decisions helped us stay aligned as a team.
  • What did not work as well? Our timeline limited the depth of website development and meant that some ideas (like a fully independent Arduino-powered system) remained at the proof-of-concept stage.
  • What did I learn as a UX designer? I learned how critical making can bridge theory and practice, how important accessibility is from the very first sketches, and how group decision-making shapes the final experience.
  • How does the process support my evolution in UX? These projects strengthened my skills in storytelling, prototyping, and reflective evaluation. They showed me that UX design is not only about screens but also about tangible objects, materials, and the communities that form around them.

Concluding Evaluation of the Final Design

Overall, the Mini Sprout Box successfully reimagines a simple cardboard box as an accessible, low-maintenance gardening tool. The combination of recycled materials, self-watering features, and a compact footprint makes it especially suited to users with limited space or mobility. Our GMP2 enhancements — audio and visual cues, Braille, improved circulation, and a community-oriented website concept — strengthened the project’s alignment with sustainability and inclusivity.

At the same time, the project surfaced important next steps: completing the digital platform, refining the electronics into a fully standalone module, and deciding whether the product should be sold as a ready-made box, a DIY kit, or both. These open questions remind me that UX design is an ongoing process of learning with users, not a single finished object.
